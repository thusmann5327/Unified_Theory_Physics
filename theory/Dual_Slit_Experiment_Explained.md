## Abstract

The double-slit experiment is the central mystery of quantum mechanics. A single particle, fired at two slits, produces an interference pattern — as if it traveled through both slits simultaneously. Place a detector at either slit and the pattern vanishes — the particle goes through one slit only. No interpretation of quantum mechanics has provided a physical mechanism for why observation changes the outcome.

The Husmann Decomposition resolves this. The particle does not "choose" a path. The detector does not "collapse a wavefunction." What happens is a geometric projection — a 5→3 collapse of the Cantor spectrum — triggered by the act of coupling the particle's bracket address to a σ₃ detector. The interference pattern is not destroyed by observation. It is moved into the dark sector, where the observer's retina cannot follow.

This document walks through the double-slit experiment step by step within the framework.

The framework resolves all three by identifying what an observer IS, what a measurement DOES, and where the interference goes when it disappears.

**Framework explanation:** The 5→3 collapse is not a local event in time. The Cantor spectrum is a spatial structure — the bracket addresses exist across the full lattice simultaneously. If the detector is removed before the conduit paths are fully absorbed into the expanded dark-sector walls, the five-sector state is restored. The extended eigenstate reforms.
