## What Lineweaver and Patel Did

Lineweaver and Patel plotted every known object in the universe — from neutrinos to galaxy superclusters — on a single log(mass) vs log(radius) diagram. Two boundary lines create forbidden triangular regions where no objects can exist:

1. **The Schwarzschild line** (slope +1 in log-log): $r_s = 2Gm/c^2$. Below this, objects are denser than black holes. All black holes, from Planck-mass instantons to supermassive galactic centers, sit on this line. The Hubble radius (the observable universe) also sits on it.

2. **The Compton line** (slope −1 in log-log): $\lambda_c = \hbar/(mc)$. Below this, quantum uncertainty prevents objects from being localized. All fundamental particles (quarks, electrons, neutrinos) sit on this line.

These two lines are **orthogonal** — slope +1 and slope −1 form a right angle in log-log space. They intersect at the **Planck point** $(l_P, m_P)$, the smallest possible object: a Planck-mass black hole, or "instanton."

All observable matter exists in the triangular region **between** these two forbidden zones. The atomic density isoline (slope 3, since $m \propto \rho r^3$) runs through this allowed region, with atoms, life, planets, stars, and galaxies all clustered near it.

## The Factor of 2: Lineweaver's Open Question

Lineweaver and Patel note an important subtlety: the Schwarzschild radius of a Planck-mass black hole is $r_s(m_P) = 2l_P$, not $l_P$. The two lines don't quite cross at $(l_P, m_P)$ — there's a factor of 2.

They resolve this by noting that for a **maximally rotating Kerr black hole**, the two horizons (inner Cauchy and outer) merge at $r_\pm = r_s/2 = l_P$. The Compton wavelength and the averaged Kerr horizon then cross exactly at $(l_P, m_P)$.

In the Husmann framework, this factor of 2 has a natural interpretation:

$$r_s = 2 \times \frac{Gm}{c^2} \quad \longleftrightarrow \quad \text{TWO fold planes}$$

The baryon formation mechanism requires matter to exist at the intersection of **two** fold planes (silver × gold). The factor of 2 in the Schwarzschild radius counts fold planes. A single fold plane gives $Gm/c^2$ (dark matter, confined on one axis). Two fold planes give $2Gm/c^2$ (baryonic matter, confined on both axes). This is the same counting that produces $W^4$ for the baryon fraction: four gap boundaries from two fold planes.

The Kerr resolution (averaging inner and outer horizons) maps onto the framework's two-horizon structure: the inner wall (σ₂) and outer wall (σ₄) of each Cantor node. Their average $(σ₂ + σ₄)/2 = (0.235 + 0.559)/2 = 0.397 = σ_{\text{shell}}$ — the shell center, which IS the probability peak.
