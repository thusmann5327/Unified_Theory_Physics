## 5. The Look-Elsewhere Problem

### 5.1 The Concern

With ~15,000 combinations of framework building blocks and 15+ target ratios, the probability of finding SOME matches at < 0.1% by chance is non-trivial. This is the look-elsewhere effect — the same issue that killed 57 out of 68 matches in the Theorem paper's original cross-scale scan.

A Monte Carlo validation is essential. Until 100,000 random frameworks are tested against the same targets with the same search space, the 18 sub-0.1% matches cannot be claimed as predictions.

### 5.2 Why Some Matches Are Stronger Than Others

Not all matches are equally vulnerable to the look-elsewhere effect:

**Koide = ν is NOT a random match.** The Hausdorff dimension D_s = 1/2 is a topological invariant of the specific Cantor set generated by the AAH Hamiltonian at critical coupling. The correlation length exponent ν = 1/(2 − D_s) = 2/3 follows from this invariant. The Koide formula equaling ν connects a SPECIFIC spectral property (the fractal dimension) to a SPECIFIC physical observable (the lepton mass sum rule). This has a mechanism — the three lepton masses are eigenvalue positions constrained by the fractal measure — even if the full derivation is missing.

**t/c = 136 shares structure with gravity.** The gravity hierarchy ALSO uses 4 × F(9) = 136. This cross-domain sharing — the same framework expression appearing in two independent physical domains — is extremely unlikely by chance. A random framework might match t/c = 136 by accident, but it won't ALSO use 136 for its gravity hierarchy.

**c/u = 2N shares structure with α.** The bracket count N appears in both the fine structure constant (α⁻¹ = N × W) and the charm-up ratio (c/u = 2N). Again: cross-domain sharing that random frameworks cannot reproduce.

### 5.3 What the Monte Carlo Must Show

The Monte Carlo must test not just hit counts but **cross-domain sharing**: how many random frameworks simultaneously match particle ratios AND use the SAME constants for their cosmological predictions? The real framework's Koide = ν, t/c = gravity exponent, and c/u = 2 × α-bracket are cross-domain links. Random frameworks will match some ratios but won't show this structural interconnection.

## 6. What This Does NOT Explain

We present these results with full honesty about their limitations:

**No derivation of absolute masses.** The framework predicts mass RATIOS, not absolute values. Why the electron weighs 0.511 MeV rather than 5.11 MeV is not addressed. This is analogous to the atomic radius framework predicting vdW/cov RATIOS but needing the Bohr radius to set the scale.

**No mechanism for quark confinement.** The statement "the top quark sits 136 channels above the charm quark" is descriptive, not mechanistic. The framework does not derive QCD or explain why quarks confine into hadrons.

**No derivation of the Aufbau principle for particles.** Just as the atomic framework uses empirical electron configurations, the particle framework uses empirical masses. A true derivation would start from the spectrum and predict which eigenstates become fermions with which masses.

**Products of three constants offer combinatorial flexibility.** With 46 building blocks and products of up to 3, there are ~15,000 combinations. Finding 18 matches to 15 targets at 0.1% requires roughly 1 in 800 combinations to hit — not astronomically unlikely. The Monte Carlo must quantify this.
