## 7. The Tube: Better Than a Wire

The original Ellie's Transit design used a solid thorium wire with a quasicrystal coating on the outside. The improved design uses a **hollow thorium tube** with QC coating on the inside AND outside.

### Why Hollow?

**Reason 1: Phonon resonant cavity.**

In a solid wire, lattice vibrations (phonons) scatter randomly through the bulk material. Energy spreads out in all directions. There's no buildup.

In a hollow tube with QC on the inner wall, phonons bounce back and forth between the opposing QC surfaces. This creates **standing waves** — specific frequencies where the phonon amplitude builds up over many reflections, just like sound waves in an organ pipe or light waves in a laser cavity.

The quality factor Q of this cavity tells you how many times a phonon bounces before losing its energy. Higher Q means more energy buildup. A solid wire has Q ≈ 1 (no bouncing). A well-designed tube cavity can have Q = 100 to 10,000.

```
Solid wire:  phonons scatter randomly → Q ≈ 1
Hollow tube: phonons bounce in cavity → Q = 100 to 10,000
                                         ↑
                              This amplifies the entire energy chain
```

**Reason 2: Hydrogen fuel delivery.**

In a solid wire design, hydrogen gas can only reach the boron-11 coating from the outside. Protons must penetrate inward through the coating to reach the thorium-QC interface.

In a hollow tube, hydrogen flows through the center channel, making direct contact with the inner surface. The protons are right where the action is.

**Reason 3: Helium-4 detection.**

The hollow tube is a flow-through reactor: hydrogen in one end, helium out the other. You can measure the reaction rate by putting a mass spectrometer on the exhaust and counting helium-4 atoms. This is unambiguous proof of nuclear reaction — no false positives.

With a solid wire, you can only measure heat (calorimetry), which could have chemical explanations. With a tube, you measure nuclear products directly.

## 12. What Limits the Lifetime

The fuel (B-11) is not the limit. 100 grams lasts 145 years. The limit is the physical hardware:

### QC Coating Degradation (5-20 years)

The quasicrystal coating faces hydrogen gas on the inner surface and thermal cycling during operation. Over years, the coating may:
- Slowly oxidize at the surface
- Develop microcracks from thermal expansion mismatch
- Lose crystallinity at grain boundaries

**Mitigation:** The QC coating can be reapplied without replacing the thorium tube. Think of it like repainting a house — you refresh the surface layer periodically.

### Thorium Tube Hydrogen Embrittlement (10-50 years)

Hydrogen atoms can slowly diffuse into metal lattices, collecting at grain boundaries and creating internal pressure that leads to cracking (embrittlement). However:
- ThO₂ naturally forms on thorium surfaces and is an excellent hydrogen barrier
- The tube wall is thick (5 μm) relative to the hydrogen diffusion zone
- Operating temperature can be optimized to minimize diffusion

**Mitigation:** Replace the tube module every 10-50 years. It's a cartridge swap.

### Radiation Damage (>100 years)

Alpha particles from the reaction cause atomic displacements in the surrounding material. At 20 kW, the displacement rate is low because alphas deposit most of their energy in the B-11 coating (which is the INTENDED target) rather than in the thorium wall.

**This is not the limiting factor** at any practical power level.
